Finding Serenity in the Swiss Alps

March 15, 2025 Allyssa Mae Felipe 8 min read

There's something magical about standing atop a mountain, breathing in crisp air that feels like it's cleansing your very soul. My trek through the Swiss Alps last month wasn't just about conquering physical challenges—it was a journey of reconnecting with myself in the vastness of nature.

The adventure began in Zermatt, a car-free village nestled at the foot of the iconic Matterhorn. As I stepped off the train, I was immediately struck by the clean mountain air and the imposing silhouette of the pyramid-shaped peak looming over the town. Wooden chalets with flower-adorned balconies lined the streets, creating a picture-perfect alpine setting.

The Call of the Mountains

After settling into my cozy guesthouse, I spent the first day acclimating to the altitude and planning my hiking routes. The local guides recommended starting with the Five Lakes Trail, a moderate hike that offers spectacular views of the Matterhorn reflected in crystal-clear alpine lakes.

Dawn broke with clear skies on my first hiking day—a good omen. Armed with a map, water, and a camera, I took the Sunnegga funicular up to the starting point. The initial climb was steep, but the moment I reached the ridge and caught my first glimpse of Stellisee, one of the five lakes, all fatigue vanished. The still water perfectly mirrored the Matterhorn, creating a double image so perfect it seemed unreal.

"Mountains teach us that our problems are smaller than we imagine them to be."

As I continued along the trail, each lake offered a different perspective of the surrounding peaks. Grindjisee, bordered by pine trees, provided a framed view of the mountains. Grünsee's emerald waters contrasted beautifully with the rugged terrain. By the time I reached Moosjisee, with its unusual blue-green color created by glacial minerals, I felt a deep sense of peace settling within me.

Unexpected Connections

The beauty of solo travel is that you're never truly alone unless you choose to be. At Leisee, the final lake on the trail, I met a local shepherd who had been tending his flock in these mountains for over forty years. As we shared lunch on a sun-warmed rock, he pointed out rare alpine flowers and told stories of how climate change had altered the landscape over his lifetime.

This unexpected encounter reminded me why I travel: not just to see beautiful places, but to connect with the people who call these places home, who understand the land in ways visitors rarely can. His stories added layers of meaning to the landscape that I would have missed on my own.

Finding Stillness

The following days brought more challenging hikes, including a partial ascent of the Gornergrat ridge and a day trip to the Monte Rosa glacier. Each step took me further from the digital noise and constant connectivity that dominated my everyday life.

On my third day, I discovered a small plateau overlooking the valley. It was nothing special according to the map—not a famous viewpoint or landmark—but something drew me to linger there. Sitting on a sun-warmed rock, I found myself doing something I hadn't done in years: absolutely nothing. No checking phones, no taking photos, no planning the next activity. Just being present with the mountains, the sky, and my own thoughts.

That hour of stillness became the most transformative part of my journey. In the silence, thoughts and feelings I had been too busy to acknowledge surfaced naturally. Decisions that had seemed complicated became clear. Priorities realigned themselves without effort.

Lessons from the Heights

The mountains have a way of putting things in perspective. Problems that seemed overwhelming in daily life appeared manageable when viewed from 3,000 meters above sea level. The physical challenge of hiking taught me that progress doesn't always feel good in the moment—sometimes it's uncomfortable, sometimes it hurts—but the view from the top makes it worthwhile.

As I descended back to Zermatt on my final day, I carried more than photographs and souvenirs. I carried a renewed sense of clarity and purpose, along with a promise to myself: to create more spaces for stillness in my everyday life, to step away from the noise regularly and reconnect with myself.

The Swiss Alps didn't change my life in dramatic, visible ways. I didn't quit my job to become a mountain guide or move to a chalet in the woods. But the subtle internal shift—a recalibration of what matters, a reminder of my own resilience, a renewed appreciation for silence—has influenced how I approach each day since returning.

Sometimes the most meaningful journeys aren't about how far you travel, but how deeply you connect—with the world around you, with the people you meet, and most importantly, with yourself.

Have you ever experienced this kind of reconnection through travel? I'd love to hear your stories in the comments below.
